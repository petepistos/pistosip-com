include an addendum to the Incident Response plan called When to Report and Incident:
A cybersecurity incident becomes reportable to the FBI, the New York State Department of Financial Services, and the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services Office for Civil Rights based on different triggers, data types, and enforcement expectations, and those differences drive both your obligations and your risk. Reporting to the FBI is not legally required but is strongly recommended when the incident involves criminal activity such as ransomware, wire fraud, business email compromise, data theft, or an active intrusion. The FBI does not define “reportable data” in regulatory terms; instead, it focuses on evidence of a crime or operational impact, including stolen funds, compromised credentials, exfiltrated data tied to an attack, or indicators of ongoing threat activity. There are no direct penalties for failing to report, but not doing so can materially limit fund recovery, weaken cyber insurance claims, and eliminate access to law enforcement support.

By contrast, reporting to NY DFS is mandatory for regulated entities and carries real enforcement exposure. An incident must be reported within 72 hours if it is likely to materially harm operations, involves ransomware or significant system compromise, or triggers notification to another regulator. The relevant data standard is Nonpublic Information (NPI), which includes personally identifiable information (such as Social Security numbers or driver’s license numbers), financial account and transaction data, authentication credentials, and business-sensitive information that could impact the organization if exposed. If NPI is accessed, exfiltrated, or reasonably believed to be at risk in a way that could affect the business or its customers, it is effectively reportable. Penalties for noncompliance include regulatory fines, formal enforcement actions, and reputational damage, particularly during annual certification reviews. In practice, DFS evaluates not just the incident but the strength of your controls, documentation, and response process, and weak governance often drives the outcome more than the breach itself.

HIPAA reporting is narrower but more rigid, triggered specifically by the exposure or unauthorized access to Protected Health Information (PHI). PHI includes any health-related information—such as medical records, diagnoses, treatment details, or insurance and billing data—when tied to an identifiable individual through names, dates of birth, addresses, or similar identifiers. Even partial datasets can qualify if re-identification is possible. A breach is presumed reportable unless a documented risk assessment demonstrates a low probability that the data was compromised. If reportable, affected individuals must be notified within 60 days, and breaches involving 500 or more individuals must also be reported to HHS within that same timeframe, while smaller breaches are reported annually. Penalties range from $100 to $50,000 per violation, with annual caps exceeding $1.5 million per category, along with potential corrective action plans and public disclosure of larger breaches. In practice, enforcement actions are most often driven by failures in risk assessment, documentation, and timely notification rather than the breach event itself.

Taken together, the decision logic is straightforward: if the incident involves criminal activity or financial impact, report to the FBI; if you are DFS-regulated and Nonpublic Information or operational integrity is at risk, report to NY DFS within 72 hours; and if PHI is exposed or accessed without authorization, assume HIPAA reporting unless you can clearly and defensibly prove otherwise.
